Rosana Paulino

Systemic Memory, Botanical Metaphor, and the Black Female Body.

The Artist and The Archive

Born in 1967 in São Paulo, Brazil, Rosana Paulino’s visual language is inextricably linked to her upbringing in a working-class family, surrounded by women engaged in the domestic labor of sewing. This early exposure transformed the thread from a tool of domesticity into a profound conceptual instrument. As the first Black woman to earn a PhD in Visual Arts from the University of São Paulo, her work operates at the intersection of material practice and rigorous academic research.

Her portfolio actively deconstructs 19th-century European pseudoscientific archives—such as the ethnographic photography generated by the Agassiz expedition—which sought to biologically classify and subordinate Black bodies. Paulino utilizes the "suture" to mend these violent historical fractures, rejecting the colonial gaze and reclaiming agency over Afro-Brazilian representation.

Jatobás Series (Gêmeas)

Produced during a period of intensifying environmental exploitation and socio-political polarization in Brazil, the Jatobás series represents a sharp analytical pivot toward ecofeminism and structural critique. Paulino merges human anatomy with the robust, rhizomatic roots of the sacred Brazilian Jatobá tree.

By reappropriating the detached visual language of classical botanical illustrations, she challenges biological determinism. The exposed roots do not signify displacement or vulnerability; rather, they serve as a visual taxonomy of survival, positioning the Black woman as the immovable, foundational anchor of the socio-ecological landscape.

"My work is a suture."

- Rosana Paulino
